CPR

- If someone is unconscious and not breathing normally, begin CPR.
- Place the heel of your hand on the center of the person's chest.
- Place your other hand on top of the first hand, interlocking your fingers.
- Perform chest compressions by pushing down at least 2 inches at a rate of 100 to 120 compressions per minute.
- After 30 compressions, give two rescue breaths. Tilt the person's head back slightly and lift the chin to open the airway.
Pinch the nose shut and give two breaths, each lasting about 1 second.
- Continue CPR until medical help arrives or the person shows signs of life.

Heimlich Maneuver

- If someone is choking and unable to breathe or speak, stand behind the person and wrap your arms around their waist.
- Make a fist with one hand and place the thumb side against the person's abdomen, just above the navel and below the ribcage.
- Grasp your fist with your other hand and give quick, upward thrusts into the abdomen.
- Repeat thrusts until the object causing the choking is dislodged or the person becomes unconscious.

Treating Cuts and Wounds:

- Wash your hands thoroughly with soap and water.
- Apply pressure to the wound using a clean cloth or bandage to stop any bleeding.
- Clean the wound gently with soap and water to prevent infection.
- Apply an antibiotic ointment to the wound and cover it with a sterile bandage or dressing

Treating Burns

- Immediately cool the burn with running water for at least 10 minutes to reduce pain and swelling.
- Remove any clothing or jewelry from the burned area unless it's stuck to the skin.
- Cover the burn with a sterile gauze bandage or a clean cloth.
- Do not apply ice, butter, or ointments to the burn, as they can make it worse.

Dealing with Nosebleeds:

- Have the person sit down and lean forward slightly.
- Pinch the nostrils together with your thumb and index finger and breathe through the mouth.
- Apply continuous pressure for about 10 minutes, then release and see if the bleeding has stopped.
- If bleeding persists, repeat the process or seek medical attention.

Treating Sprains and Strains

li> Treating Sprains and Strains:
 - Rest the injured area and avoid putting weight on it.
- Apply ice wrapped in a cloth for 15-20 minutes every 2-3 hours to reduce swelling and pain.
- Compress the area with an elastic bandage to provide support and minimize swelling.
- Elevate the injured limb above heart level to reduce swelling.
- If pain persists or if there's severe swelling, seek medical attention.

Managing Nosebleeds:

- Have the person sit down and lean forward slightly.
- Pinch the soft part of the nose, just below the bridge, and hold for 5-10 minutes.
- Encourage the person to breathe through their mouth.
- Apply an ice pack or cold compress to the bridge of the nose to constrict blood vessels and reduce bleeding.
- If bleeding persists after 15-20 minutes of pressure, seek medical help.

Dealing with Bee Stings or Insect Bites:

- If a stinger is visible, remove it by scraping it off with a blunt object like a credit card.
- Wash the area with soap and water.
- Apply a cold compress or ice pack to reduce swelling and pain.
- Use over-the-counter antihistamines or anti-itch creams to relieve itching.
- Monitor for signs of allergic reaction such as difficulty breathing, swelling of the face or throat, and seek emergency medical help if they occur.

Managing Heat Exhaustion:

- Move the person to a cooler place with shade or air conditioning.
- Loosen clothing and apply cool, wet cloths to the skin.
- Encourage the person to drink cool water or sports drinks to rehydrate.
- Elevate the person's feet slightly.
- If symptoms worsen or if the person loses consciousness, seek medical attention immediately.

Treating Minor Eye Injuries:

- Flush the eye with clean water for at least 15 minutes to remove any foreign objects.
- Avoid rubbing the eye, as it may cause further damage.
- If there's an object embedded in the eye, do not attempt to remove it; seek immediate medical help.
- Cover the injured eye with a sterile dressing or clean cloth.
- Seek medical attention if there's persistent pain, redness, or vision changes.

Managing Fractures or Broken Bones:

- Keep the injured area still and support it to prevent further injury.
- If there's an open wound, cover it with a sterile dressing.
- Apply ice wrapped in a cloth to reduce swelling and pain.
- Immobilize the injured limb using a splint or makeshift splint (such as a rolled-up newspaper or a stick) to prevent movement.

Treating Heat Stroke

- Move the person to a cooler area and remove excess clothing.
- Cool the person rapidly by applying ice packs or cold cloths to the armpits, groin, neck, and back.
- Fan the person or create air circulation to enhance cooling.
- Offer sips of water if the person is conscious and able to swallow.
- Seek emergency medical help as heat stroke is a life-threatening condition.

Dealing with Allergic Reactions (Anaphylaxis):

- Administer an epinephrine auto-injector (EpiPen) if available, following the instructions provided.
- Call emergency services immediately.
- Help the person sit upright and stay calm.
- If the person has difficulty breathing, assist with any prescribed medications such as an inhaler.
- Monitor the person's vital signs until medical help arrives.

Handling Seizures:

- Keep the person safe from injury by moving nearby objects out of the way.
- Cushion the person's head and loosen tight clothing around the neck.
- Do not restrain the person's movements or put anything in their mouth.
- Time the duration of the seizure.
- After the seizure, help the person into a comfortable position and offer reassurance. If the seizure lasts longer than 5 minutes, call emergency services.
.

Managing Poisoning:

- Call emergency services immediately.
- If the person is conscious, try to identify the substance ingested.
- Do not induce vomiting unless advised by a medical professional.
- If the poison was inhaled, move the person to fresh air.
- Follow any specific instructions given by emergency services until help arrives.

In case of emergency, call '108' or '104' or your local emergency number.
